Ancient Systemic Disease Project




About

Systemic diseases are a devastating global health burden and are expressed differently across populations in association with their unique evolutionary, social, and biological trajectories. Understanding the individual evolutionary, environmental, and social histories of systemic diseases in specific populations would be invaluable for the development of effective structural interventions for modern populations. Yet, macroscopic skeletal data alone is insufficient for the population-level paleoepidemiological investigation of the majority of systemic diseases. At present, there are no definitive methods by which the presence of conditions such as type 1 diabetes (T1D), rheumatoid arthritis (RA), cardiovascular diseases (CVD), certain types of malnutrition, and psychological disorders can be reliably assessed in human skeletal samples. In modern populations, however, these conditions have been strongly demonstrated to correlate with specific characteristics of the human oral microbiome. Due to the frequent presence and remarkable preservation of dental calculus, or calcified dental plaque, in preindustralized populations, the human oral microbiome can be assessed in archaeological samples. Using Next Generation Sequencing (NGS), ancient DNA (aDNA) associated with human oral microbiomes can be extracted from dental calculus and analyzed alongside paleodemographic and paleopathological data to assess the impact of systemic diseases in archaeological populations. The goal of this project is to develop and evaluate this emerging methodology by addressing three major aims:

(1) developing predictive risk models for specific systemic diseases (T1D, RA, CVD, malnutrition, mental health) by leveraging published modern oral microbiome studies and datasets.

(2) assessing the utility of these models in a well-studied archaeological population from medieval and post-medieval London (n = 260) and determining whether predicted risk correlates with mortality and morbidity skeletal characteristics.

(3) applying the risk models to an expanded Middle Eastern dataset bridging agricultural and demographic transitions in order to re-evaluate long-standing questions and assumptions about how significant changes in human history impacted our health.
